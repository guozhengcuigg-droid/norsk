News 1: Historic World Cup Quarter-Final – Norway Faces England Tonight at 22:00

Norway is playing in a World Cup quarter-final for the first time in 28 years. The last time Norway was in a World Cup quarter-final was in 1998. Tonight at 22:00 Norwegian time, the Norwegian team will face England. The match will be broadcast on NRK, and millions of Norwegians will be following along in front of their TV screens. This is without a doubt one of the biggest football matches in Norwegian history. The entire nation will stop to watch this game.

Norway's journey to the quarter-final has been exciting. In the group stage, Norway won against Iraq and Senegal but lost to France. In the round of 16, Norway faced Ivory Coast and won 2-1. Then came the big surprise: in the quarter-final, Norway beat Brazil 2-1. Erling Braut Haaland scored both goals for Norway. This was a sensational achievement, as Brazil is one of the world's best football teams and has won the World Cup five times.

The match against England will be a tough challenge. England is a strong team with many good players. They beat Croatia 4-2 in the group stage and have shown good form throughout the tournament. But the Norwegian team has also shown that they can beat big nations. After the victory over Brazil, confidence is high in the Norwegian squad. The national team coach has said that the team is entering the match without fear and that they believe in victory.

In Bergen, the atmosphere is absolutely electric. Bergen municipality is inviting people to a free screening of the match in Nygårdsparken. Big screens will be set up so that everyone can watch the match together outdoors. This is a public celebration that brings together Bergen residents of all ages. In other cities such as Oslo and Trondheim, large public viewing events with big screens are also planned. Interest in the national team has been enormous in recent days. Outside sports shops in Oslo, the queue has been several hundred metres long to secure a Norwegian national team jersey. At the same time, the police in Bergen are preparing for a big celebration if Norway wins. Regardless of the result, this will be a historic evening for Norwegian football, and many will remember this day for the rest of their lives.

News 2: AG Tunnel Bankrupt – 80 Employees Lose Their Jobs in Sogndal

The tunnel construction company AG Tunnel in Sogndal has gone bankrupt. The company had approximately 80 employees, and all of them are now losing their jobs. This is a heavy day for the employees and for the local community in Sogndal, which is located in Vestland county. AG Tunnel specialised in tunnel rehabilitation, water and frost protection, and the company has been an important employer in the region for many years.

AG Tunnel's problems began several months ago. In mid-June, the company announced that it would undergo a restructuring. Restructuring means that a company tries to sort out its finances without going bankrupt. Unfortunately, AG Tunnel was unable to do this. Managing director Frode Johan Bjørdal says that the goal all along was to save the company, but they did not succeed. He describes the situation as incredibly sad for everyone involved.

An important cause of the bankruptcy is a conflict with another contractor, Bertelsen og Garpestad, which also went bankrupt earlier. The two companies worked together on upgrading two tunnels on the E134 highway over Haukelifjell. Haukelifjell is an important mountain pass between Eastern and Western Norway. A dispute arose between the companies, and the case ended up in court. AG Tunnel won the lawsuit and was to receive around 30 million Norwegian kroner in compensation from Bertelsen og Garpestad.

The problem was that Bertelsen og Garpestad itself had gone bankrupt before the compensation was paid out. Therefore, AG Tunnel never received the money they were entitled to. Without these 30 million kroner, AG Tunnel was unable to continue operations. Board chairman Frode Michal Nilsen says that this was "the final nail in the coffin" for AG Tunnel. Without the compensation money, it was simply not possible to save the company.

The bankruptcy has major consequences for the local community in Sogndal. Eighty jobs are disappearing, and many families are directly affected. Sogndal is a medium-sized municipality with around 8,000 inhabitants, so the loss of such a large employer is keenly felt. The employees must now find new jobs, and some may have to move to find work. The work on the E134 tunnels may also be delayed, because the Norwegian Public Roads Administration must now find new contractors to take over the assignments. This is a reminder of how vulnerable the business sector can be, especially in smaller municipalities.
