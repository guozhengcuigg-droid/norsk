News 1: Bergen Municipality Convicted of Illegal Retaliation Against Whistleblower

A ruling from Hordaland District Court has made headlines in Bergen this week. Bergen Municipality has been convicted of illegal retaliation against a safety representative who blew the whistle on problematic conditions at the workplace.

The case concerns a safety representative at Kirkebirkeland residential care facility in Fana. A safety representative (verneombod) is a person responsible for ensuring that the work environment is safe and healthy for all employees. Last autumn, this person reported concerns to the Norwegian Labour Inspection Authority about conditions at the workplace that he considered problematic. Shortly afterwards, he lost his job.

The safety representative decided to sue Bergen Municipality. He believed he was fired as a direct punishment for whistleblowing. This is illegal in Norway — employers cannot punish employees who speak up about wrongdoing. This is called retaliation, and it is protected by the Working Environment Act.

The court has now reached its conclusion. Hordaland District Court has ruled that Bergen Municipality subjected the safety representative to illegal retaliation on two specific occasions. The municipality must pay 25,000 kroner in compensation to the safety representative. Compensation (oppreisning) is a sum of money awarded as reparation for an injustice one has suffered.

However, the safety representative does not get his job back. The majority of the court found that the municipality had justifiable grounds for the dismissal. The reason was that the safety representative had used patient information in his whistleblowing reports, which breached the duty of confidentiality. Duty of confidentiality (taushetsplikt) means you cannot share information about other people without permission.

The safety representative's lawyer, Helene Frich Hanøy, says her client is satisfied that the court confirmed he was subjected to retaliation by his employer. She adds that he hopes the ruling can contribute to better treatment of whistleblowers in the future. The case shows how complex whistleblowing can be — you have the right to speak up, but you must also follow privacy regulations.

News 2: OpenAI Launches New and More Powerful AI Model — GPT-5.6

The technology company OpenAI, the creator of ChatGPT, has announced that its newest and most powerful AI models will become available to the public as early as Thursday this week. The models are called GPT-5.6 Sol, Terra, and Luna.

The launch comes after US authorities gave the green light. There has been much discussion about how powerful AI models should be, and whether they could cause problems if they fall into the wrong hands. OpenAI itself previously said they were concerned about what the models could do.

The new GPT-5.6 family consists of three different models. Sol is the most powerful, Terra is the mid-range model, and Luna is the smallest and most affordable. They have different levels of capability and pricing, so both large companies and private individuals can choose the one that suits them best.

OpenAI is not alone in launching powerful AI models. Competitor Anthropic has already released similar models. Competition among AI companies is becoming increasingly fierce, and the technology is developing at a pace that many find both exciting and frightening.

For ordinary users, this means that AI tools are becoming ever more capable. They can help with everything from writing texts and translating languages to analyzing data and creating images. At the same time, this development raises new questions: How should we use this technology safely? And who gets to decide the rules?
