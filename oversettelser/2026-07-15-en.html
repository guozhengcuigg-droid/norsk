Today's News – July 15, 2026 (English Translation)

Hello and happy Wednesday! Today we have two exciting stories from Bergen. The first is something that makes all drivers happy – the petrol price has dropped dramatically. The second is about a Bergen company receiving major funding to test electric aircraft. Let's read more!

News 1: Price war pushes petrol below 15 kroner per liter in Bergen

On Wednesday morning, drivers in Bergen were greeted with a very pleasant surprise. Several petrol stations in the city were selling petrol for under 15 kroner per liter. The lowest price was 14.79 kroner for a liter of 95 octane. This is the cheapest petrol in all of Norway on Wednesday morning. The stations offering these low prices are located in Søreide, Kokstad and Blomsterdalen. All of these areas lie south of Bergen city center, near Flesland Airport.

Why is the petrol price so low right now? There are two main reasons. Firstly, there is a local price war between the petrol stations in this area. A price war means that stations compete for customers by lowering prices. When one station drops its price, the others must follow to avoid losing customers. Secondly, government fuel tax cuts were introduced in March this year. The government decided to reduce taxes to help people cope with high living costs. These two factors together have pushed the price below 15 kroner.

The difference between Bergen and the rest of the country is significant. While people in Bergen can fill their tanks for under 15 kroner per liter, drivers in other parts of Norway must pay much more. In Vadsø in Finnmark and Surnadal in Møre og Romsdal, a liter of petrol costs 21.60 kroner. That is over 6 kroner more per liter. For a typical car with a 50-liter tank, this means a price difference of over 300 kroner for a full tank. That is money that can be spent on other things this summer.

For many Norwegians, summer is a time for road trips. Many families drive long distances to visit relatives, go to their cabins, or experience new places. When petrol prices are low, road trips become much cheaper. For people in Bergen planning to drive over the mountains to Eastern Norway, or south along the coast, this means their holiday budget suddenly gets a small boost. It is not only individuals who benefit from low petrol prices. Companies involved in transport and delivery also see lower costs. In the long run, this could lead to lower prices for goods transported by road.

Experts say that price wars like this one usually do not last very long. Petrol stations cannot sell petrol at a loss for an extended period. Therefore, many recommend that drivers fill their tanks now while prices are low. It is also worth noting that petrol prices in Norway fluctuate a lot. Prices can change several times in the course of a single day. So it pays to monitor prices and fill up when they are at their lowest. Many Norwegians use apps and websites to compare petrol prices in real time.

News 2: Bergen company receives 30 million kroner to test electric amphibious aircraft

A Bergen company has received major financial support to develop the aircraft of the future. The company is called NOEMI Aerospace and is based in Bergen. It has been granted 30 million kroner from Innovation Norway. This is a government body that provides support for innovative projects in Norwegian business. The grant is the highest awarded to a single project so far this year. The money will be used to test the company's first electric amphibious aircraft.

NOEMI Aerospace is working on something truly special. They are building what could become the world's first certified electric amphibious aircraft for commercial passenger transport. An amphibious aircraft is a plane that can land and take off on both water and land. This means the aircraft does not need a conventional airport with a long runway. It can use fjords, lakes and rivers as take-off and landing sites. This is particularly useful in Norway, where we have lots of water and many small communities without airports. The company was previously called Elfly Group but changed its name to NOEMI Aerospace in February this year. NOEMI stands for "no emissions."

The 30 million kroner from Innovation Norway will finance the flight test program for the first aircraft. This is one of the most critical phases in developing a new aircraft. The program includes testing on the ground, on water, and in the air. The testing will take place at NOEMI's development and test facility at Torp Sandefjord Airport. The test results will document the aircraft's performance characteristics. This documentation is necessary to get the aircraft certified and approved for commercial operation. Without certification, you cannot transport passengers in regular scheduled service.

The construction of the test aircraft is already in full swing. Wing production is well underway, and motors, inverters and battery systems are in place. An inverter is a component that converts direct current from the batteries into alternating current that the motors can use. In August, the test rig for the electric powertrain will be put into operation. The goal is to roll the test aircraft out of the hangar in about twelve months. The company plans to conduct the first flight by the end of 2027. These are ambitious goals, but the company has already made significant progress in development.

The aircraft is not only designed for passenger transport. The platform can also be used for many other purposes. It can, for example, be used for cargo transport, air ambulance services, search and rescue operations, military missions, and firefighting. This makes the aircraft attractive to a wide range of customers. Founder and CEO Eric Lithun says they are not just building a new aircraft – they are building a new Norwegian industry. He believes that the support from Innovation Norway sends a clear signal to investors and industry partners that this technology has significant international potential.
