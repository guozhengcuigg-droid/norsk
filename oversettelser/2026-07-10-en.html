Today's News – July 10, 2026 (English Translation)

Hello and happy Friday! Today we have two dramatic news stories, both about safety — one about digital safety and privacy, the other about physical safety in the air. Ikea has experienced a major data breach in which tens of thousands of Norwegian customers and employees are affected. At the same time, a Ryanair flight had to make an emergency landing in Greece after a window came loose and a passenger was nearly sucked out of the aircraft. Let us take a closer look at both stories!

News 1: Data Breach at Ikea — 45,000 Personal Records Compromised

Furniture giant Ikea has been hit by a major data breach. Around 45,000 personal records of customers and employees in Norway have been compromised. The information about the attack first became known on Friday, July 10, after the news agency NTB gained access to Ikea's deviation report to the Norwegian Data Protection Authority (Datatilsynet). This is a serious case that affects many Norwegians, and the Data Protection Authority will now assess whether Ikea has complied with privacy regulations.

The data breach actually occurred as early as March 19 this year, but it took almost four months before the public learned about the case. According to the deviation report, the attack was part of a larger cyberattack against Ikea internationally. Hackers exploited a vulnerability in a software program developed by a third-party vendor and used by Ikea IT AB. This shows how vulnerable large companies can be when they use third-party software. A security expert NRK has spoken with says such attacks are becoming increasingly common.

How many people are actually affected? According to the deviation report, the hackers stole data on up to 3,261 employees and 42,370 customers at Ikea in Norway. But the actual number is probably somewhat lower, because there may be duplicates in the figures — meaning that some individuals may have been registered multiple times in the system. Still, this is a significant number of people. Those affected will be contacted by Ikea if their personal data is among what was stolen.

What kind of information is involved? The deviation report does not specify exactly what types of personal data have been compromised, but typically in such attacks, it can involve names, addresses, phone numbers, email addresses, and in the worst case, purchase history. The Data Protection Authority has strict rules for how businesses must handle personal data, and they can issue fines if a company has not had adequate security. Ikea is now cooperating with the Data Protection Authority and has also reported the matter to the police.

If you are an Ikea customer, there are some simple tips to follow. First and foremost, you should be extra attentive to emails and text messages asking for personal information — scammers can exploit such situations to send fake messages that look like they come from Ikea. You should also consider changing your password if you have an Ikea account, and keep an eye on your bank statement for suspicious transactions. Even if it has not been confirmed that banking information has been stolen, it is better to be safe than sorry.

Ikea has two large stores in Norway — one at Slependen outside Oslo and one in Åsane outside Bergen. The company also has several smaller planning studios around the country. In total, Ikea Norway has several hundred employees and hundreds of thousands of customers. Many of these may be among those affected by the data breach.

News 2: Ryanair Flight Makes Emergency Landing — Passenger Nearly Sucked Out of Window

A Ryanair flight had to make an emergency landing in Thessaloniki, Greece on Friday after a highly dramatic incident. A window on board came loose, and a male passenger was partially sucked out of the aircraft. The incident occurred while the plane was en route from Thessaloniki to Munich, Germany. According to the Reuters news agency, which cites two sources familiar with the matter, the situation was very serious, but fortunately it did not result in loss of life.

A passenger told the radio station Radio Thessaloniki what happened. The man sitting by the window was suddenly sucked out of the plane when the window came loose. His head, neck, and shoulders were outside the aircraft, but he was fortunately wearing his seatbelt. Other passengers nearby reacted quickly and held onto the man. After a while, they managed to pull him back into the plane. This was a very frightening experience for everyone on board, and it shows how important it is to always wear your seatbelt when flying.

Ryanair confirmed in a statement that a window had come loose on board the aircraft. According to the Greek news website News24/7, Ryanair says the plane landed normally, but that one passenger received medical attention as a precaution. Local media report that the man is slightly injured but in shock after the incident. He is now receiving treatment at a hospital in Thessaloniki. The other passengers were evacuated from the aircraft after the emergency landing. The flight was en route from Thessaloniki to Munich — a route used by many European tourists in the summer, including Norwegians on holiday in Greece.

How could this happen? Aircraft windows are designed to withstand large pressure differences, and it is extremely rare for a window to come loose during flight. Aviation experts say the cause could be a manufacturing defect, poor maintenance, or damage to the window that was not detected during inspection. Greek aviation authorities have launched an investigation into the incident, and Ryanair is cooperating with the authorities to find out what went wrong. The aircraft will be thoroughly inspected before it is allowed to fly again. The case is being covered by several international media outlets, including Reuters and Greek news websites.

This incident naturally raises concerns among air passengers, but it is important to remember that air travel remains one of the safest forms of transportation. According to statistics from the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO), such serious technical failures are extremely rare. Nevertheless, many passengers will probably think twice before choosing a window seat on their next flight. For Ryanair, this is a serious matter that could damage the company's reputation, especially if the investigation reveals that the company has been negligent with maintenance.
