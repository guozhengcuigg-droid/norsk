News 1: Norwegian MS Breakthrough – Cheap Medicine Could Help Patients Worldwide

Norwegian researchers have made a major breakthrough in the treatment of multiple sclerosis, also known as MS. The research project is called "Overlord" and has been carried out at Haukeland University Hospital in Bergen. The results have now been published in the prestigious New England Journal of Medicine, one of the world's most respected medical journals.

MS is a neurological disease that affects the central nervous system — that is, the brain and spinal cord. In MS, the insulation around the nerve fibers becomes damaged, causing nerve signals to be disrupted or blocked. The disease can lead to difficulty walking, numbness in the body, vision problems, and fatigue. Around 13,000 people in Norway currently live with MS.

Until now, the most effective MS medicine has cost around 300,000 Norwegian kroner per year for a single patient. This is extremely expensive, and in many poor countries, patients simply cannot afford this treatment. But now, researchers at Haukeland have shown that a much cheaper drug — rituximab — is equally effective. The price of rituximab is only around 5,000 kroner per patient per year. That is just 2 percent of the cost of the standard medication.

Research leader Øivind Torkildsen, who is a professor of neurology at the University of Bergen and a senior consultant at Haukeland, is very optimistic. He says this could have major consequences worldwide. More patients can get medicine, and they can get it earlier. This will be especially important in poor countries, where healthcare budgets are small and expensive medicine is often unavailable.

One of the patients who participated in the research project is Hanne Skarstein. She is a mother of young children and received her MS diagnosis four years ago. She says the diagnosis came as a shock, and she wasn't very positive about the future. But thanks to the research project, she quickly gained access to the new medication. Today, she lives an almost normal life. She says that without this project, she might not be here now, or be able to take care of her family.

However, not everything is positive. The researchers fear that politics and commercial interests could block the cheap medicine. The pharmaceutical industry makes big money from the expensive MS drugs, and not everyone wants a much cheaper alternative to become the standard. This is a reminder that medical research isn't just about finding the best treatments — it's also about economics and politics.

News 2: Bergen Defies Statnett – Wants Undersea Cable Instead of Overhead Power Lines

Bergen City Council has voted to investigate an alternative route for the new power line that will run between Samnanger and Øygarden. This comes despite strong warnings from Statnett, the state-owned company responsible for operating and developing Norway's power grid. Statnett believes it is urgent to get the new line in place, but the politicians in Bergen are going their own way.

The background to this case is that Western Norway needs more electricity. A lot of new industry is being planned in the region, and the existing power grid doesn't have enough capacity. Statnett has therefore planned a new power line between Samnanger and Øygarden, two municipalities west of Bergen. Samnanger lies inland, while Øygarden is an island municipality right out on the coast.

Statnett's original proposal is to build the power line as overhead lines across land — that is, large metal towers with cables hanging between them, the kind we're used to seeing in the Norwegian landscape. But Bergen City Council believes this would destroy the scenery and nature. Instead, they want an undersea cable — a cable laid on the seabed. The route they are proposing runs along Byfjorden and Herdlefjorden.

An undersea cable would be invisible to most people, and it wouldn't affect the landscape or wildlife on land. But Statnett warns that an undersea cable is much more expensive to build, and it can be harder to repair if something goes wrong. In addition, Statnett believes that investigating an alternative route will take a long time and could delay the entire project by several years.

This is a classic dilemma in Norwegian energy policy: on the one hand, we need more power to achieve the green transition and new industry. On the other hand, no one wants power lines in their own area. This is often called "NIMBY" — an English expression that stands for "Not In My Backyard." Everyone agrees we need more electricity, but no municipality wants the towers in their own jurisdiction.

The case also highlights the tension between local democracy and national interests. Should Bergen Municipality be able to decide on something that affects the entire region's power supply? Or should the state, through Statnett, be able to force through its solution? These are questions that will be debated extensively going forward, and the answer will have implications for many similar cases across the rest of the country.
