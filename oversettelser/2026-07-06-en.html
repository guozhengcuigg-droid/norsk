Today's News – July 6, 2026 (English Translation)

Good Monday! Today we look at two important stories: an environmental conflict right here in Vestland county, and a major world news event from Iran. Let's get started!

News 1: Environmental organizations demand halt in Førdefjorden – government refuses

Førdefjorden in Sunnfjord has been at the center of one of Norway's biggest environmental conflicts in recent years. Today, the conflict has reached a new level. The Norwegian Society for the Conservation of Nature (Naturvernforbundet) and Natur og Ungdom are asking Prime Minister Jonas Gahr Støre to step in and stop the dumping of mining waste in the fjord.

The background is that the Supreme Court ruled on June 17 that the permit to dump mining waste in Førdefjorden was granted illegally. The ruling was unanimous. Nevertheless, Minister of Climate and Environment Andreas Bjelland Eriksen has decided that operations can continue while authorities consider a new temporary permit.

The company Nordic Mining extracts rutile and garnet from Engebøfjellet mountain. The plan is to dump around 170 million tons of mining waste into Førdefjorden. According to environmental organizations, this will destroy life in the fjord. The government, on the other hand, believes that the fjord can tolerate at least one more year of emissions, and has stated this to ESA – the surveillance body for the EEA Agreement.

Sigrid Hoddevik Losnegård, leader of Natur og Ungdom, is strongly critical. She says the government is on a dangerous course that could damage trust in both politicians and courts. In a letter to the Prime Minister's office, the environmental organizations demand that the illegal permits be revoked.

"It is time for Støre to step in," writes Losnegård in a press release.

The case has also attracted international attention. ESA has asked the government to explain why the dumping continues after the Supreme Court declared the permit invalid. The government responds that a halt now would create uncertainty for many jobs and could weaken access to critical minerals.

The conflict in Førdefjorden is about more than just the environment. It is about what weighs heavier: nature conservation or jobs, laws or political decisions, national interests or local consequences. Many in Vestland are following the case closely, because it could have consequences for how Norwegian nature is managed in the future.

News 2: Khamenei buried in Iran – a historic era is over

In Iran, Ayatollah Ali Khamenei is being buried today, July 6. Khamenei was Iran's supreme leader for nearly 37 years, from 1989 until he was killed in a US-Israeli airstrike on February 28 this year. He was 86 years old.

Khamenei was one of the world's most powerful and controversial leaders. He held near-absolute power in Iran through the system called "velayat-e faqih" – the guardianship of the jurist. This system gives the supreme religious leader the final say in all important matters.

When Khamenei was killed in February, Iran declared 40 days of national mourning. The public ceremonies began on July 4 in Tehran, where thousands of Iranians took part in processions. Today, the mourning period concludes with the burial itself. Iranian authorities have temporarily halted all diplomatic work with the US during the ceremonies.

Reactions to Khamenei's death have been divided. Some celebrate the departure of a brutal dictator, while others mourn the loss of a religious leader. Regardless of what one thinks of Khamenei, it is clear that a historic era in Iran's history is now over. The question everyone is asking is: What happens to Iran now?

For Norway and the rest of the world, this is an important event. Iran is a key player in the Middle East, and whoever takes over power will affect both regional stability and international relations – including oil prices and security policy.
