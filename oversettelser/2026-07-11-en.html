News 1: Trump Threatens Iran with Military Strike – 1,000 Missiles Ready

US President Donald Trump has issued a very serious threat against Iran. In the early hours of Saturday, July 11, Trump wrote on his own social media platform Truth Social that the US military has 1,000 missiles ready, aimed at Iran. The background for the threat is allegations that Iran is planning to take the president's life.

Trump wrote that the missiles are "loaded and ready" and that "thousands more will follow immediately" if Iran tries to kill him. This is one of the strongest threats an American president has made against another country in many years. The statement comes as a direct reaction to reports in American media that Israel has shared intelligence with the US. According to these reports, Iran allegedly has a new and concrete plan to assassinate President Trump.

The relationship between the USA and Iran has been tense for many years. The two countries have not had diplomatic relations since 1980, following the Iranian Revolution. In recent times, the conflict has centered on Iran's nuclear program, support for militant groups in the Middle East, and attacks on American bases in the region. Trump has previously been tough in his rhetoric against Iran, but this time the threat is more direct and concrete than before.

The situation has caused great concern in the international community. Many countries and organizations are worried that a military conflict between the USA and Iran could have serious consequences for the entire Middle East region. Experts point out that an attack could lead to higher oil prices, refugee flows, and more instability in a region already marked by many conflicts. For Norway's part, such a conflict could affect both the oil economy and the international security situation.

Iran has so far not responded publicly to Trump's statements. Iranian authorities have previously denied similar allegations of assassination plans against American leaders. Analysts say the situation is very serious, but that it is still possible to avoid a military confrontation. Diplomacy and dialogue are highlighted as the best way forward by several world leaders. International law experts remind that a military attack without UN authorization may be in violation of international law.

This news comes at a time when the world is already facing several major crises. The war in Ukraine continues with full force, and in the early hours of Saturday, several people were injured in a new Russian rocket attack on Kyiv. In Venezuela, a powerful earthquake has claimed the lives of over 4,000 people. Many countries are concerned that a new conflict between the USA and Iran will make it even more difficult to handle these crises. The UN Secretary-General has previously called on all parties to show restraint and seek peaceful solutions. It is currently unclear whether the US will actually carry out an attack, or whether Trump's statements are primarily intended as a warning and a political signal.

News 2: Norwegian Cultural Heritage at Risk – Boathouses and Seaside Houses Are Disappearing

Along the Norwegian coast, an important part of our cultural heritage is at risk of disappearing forever. NRK Vestland reports that more and more boathouses (naust) and seaside houses (sjøhus) are decaying and being lost. These are buildings that have been a central part of Norwegian coastal culture for hundreds of years. Boathouses were used to store boats and fishing equipment, while seaside houses have been used for everything from trade to housing.

The problem is particularly serious in Vestland county, where many of the old wooden buildings stand without maintenance. The owners of the buildings often cannot afford to take care of them. Maintaining an old wooden house by the sea is extremely expensive. The buildings are exposed to harsh weather, salt water, and strong winds for much of the year. Many owners are pensioners or heirs who do not have the money or energy to carry out the necessary maintenance.

Cultural heritage experts say this is a national problem. Boathouses and seaside houses tell the story of Norway as a coastal nation. They show how people lived from fishing, trade, and seafaring through generations. Every building has its own story, its own architecture, and its own place in the landscape. When they disappear, we lose an important part of our shared identity and history. Experts believe it is urgent to find solutions before it is too late.

The government has some support schemes for preserving cultural heritage, but many believe the support is not sufficient. Local enthusiasts and voluntary organizations are making great efforts to save the buildings, but they cannot save everything alone. Several municipalities are now calling for more help from the state. They also want simpler rules for what owners can use the buildings for, so that it becomes more attractive to take care of them. Some suggest that boathouses could be used for tourist accommodation, or that seaside houses could be converted into cultural centers and meeting places.

Climate change makes the situation even more difficult. More frequent storms, more precipitation, and higher sea levels accelerate the rate of decay. Buildings that have stood for over 200 years can be destroyed within a few years if nothing is done. This applies not only to Norway — many coastal countries in Europe are experiencing the same problem. Researchers say we must act quickly if we want to save this cultural heritage for future generations.

For the tourism industry in Vestland, this is also an important issue. The old seaside houses and boathouses are a major attraction for visitors from all over the world. Many tourists come to Bergen and Western Norway precisely to experience the unique coastal culture. Bryggen in Bergen, which is on UNESCO's World Heritage List, is the most famous example of this building tradition. But outside the city center, there are hundreds of lesser-known buildings that also have great historical value. When these disappear, we lose not only history — we also lose an important part of what makes Norway an attractive travel destination. Several tourism operators have therefore joined the call for more state support for the preservation of coastal cultural heritage.
